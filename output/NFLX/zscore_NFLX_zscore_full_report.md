## Altman Z-Score (Original) Formula

Z = 1.2*X1 + 1.4*X2 + 3.3*X3 + 0.6*X4 + 1.0*X5
- X1 = (Current Assets - Current Liabilities) / Total Assets
- X2 = Retained Earnings / Total Assets
- X3 = EBIT / Total Assets
- X4 = Market Value of Equity / Total Liabilities
- X5 = Sales / Total Assets

## Diagnostic Evaluation of Financial Health

### Liquidity
Liquidity measures the company's ability to meet short-term obligations. A higher current ratio indicates better liquidity. For Netflix, the current ratio has shown fluctuations, suggesting potential liquidity concerns. A current ratio below 1 indicates that current liabilities exceed current assets, which could lead to cash flow issues.

### Profitability
Profitability is assessed through metrics like EBIT (Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) and retained earnings. Netflix has experienced varying EBIT margins, which are crucial for sustaining operations and funding future growth. A declining EBIT margin could signal operational inefficiencies or increased competition.

### Capital Efficiency
Capital efficiency is evaluated through the sales-to-assets ratio (X5). A higher ratio indicates effective use of assets to generate sales. Netflix's sales-to-assets ratio has been relatively stable, suggesting that the company is effectively utilizing its assets to generate revenue.

### Leverage
Leverage is assessed through the market value of equity relative to total liabilities (X4). A lower ratio indicates higher financial risk. Netflix's leverage has increased, reflecting a growing reliance on debt financing, which may pose risks if cash flows do not improve.

### Z-Score Trend Interpretation
The Z-Score provides a quantitative measure of financial health, with scores below 1.8 indicating distress. Netflix's Z-Score trend over recent quarters has shown signs of volatility, reflecting the company's challenges in maintaining profitability and managing debt levels. A declining Z-Score could indicate increasing financial distress, necessitating immediate management intervention.
